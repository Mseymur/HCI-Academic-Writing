# Level Calibration: Master's vs Doctoral Criteria and Judging

Source basis: EQF level 7 and level 8 descriptors (CEDEFOP/Europass, official EU framework governing Austrian and other European degrees); Dublin Descriptors for the second and third Bologna cycles (QF-EHEA; verified via academic secondary source quoting them — marked accordingly); Phillips & Pugh, *How to Get a PhD*, ch. 3, "Differences between the MPhil and the PhD" (read in full).

**Core principle:** the Master's–doctoral difference is not one rubric with a lower passing score. On some axes it is a threshold (depth, scope, breadth of review); on others it is a **different license** — things permitted at Master's level that are failures at doctoral level, and obligations that exist only at doctoral level. Apply the correct license, not a discounted doctoral rubric.

## 1. The official ladders

**EQF level 7 (Master's):** highly specialised knowledge, *some* of which is at the forefront of the field, as the *basis for* original thinking and research; critical awareness of knowledge issues; specialised problem-solving in research or innovation to develop new knowledge and integrate knowledge across fields.

**EQF level 8 (Doctorate):** knowledge *at the most advanced frontier* of the field and at the interface between fields; the most advanced skills, including synthesis and evaluation, required to *extend and redefine existing knowledge*; substantial authority, innovation, autonomy, scholarly integrity, sustained commitment to new ideas at the forefront.

**Dublin Descriptors (Bologna second vs third cycle)** *(verified via secondary academic source)*: Master's-level knowledge "provides a basis or opportunity for originality in the development or application of ideas, often in the context of research"; problems are solved in new or unfamiliar environments within broader contexts; judgments are formed with incomplete data. Doctoral level demonstrates systematic command of the field, proficiency in its research methods, and **a contribution of original research** — the ability to conceive, plan, implement and complete a serious research process, with critical analysis, evaluation and synthesis of new and complex ideas.

**The compression that matters for writing:** a Master's thesis demonstrates the *capacity* to do research — competent execution of the full research process with a degree of originality. A doctorate delivers an *original contribution that extends the field* and demonstrates full professional autonomy. The Master's is graded on the demonstrated process; the doctorate is graded on the contribution itself.

## 2. What is licensed at Master's level that fails at doctoral level (Phillips & Pugh, ch. 3)

- **Limited scope and degree of originality are acceptable.** The investigation may be narrower and the originality humbler than a doctorate requires.
- **Replication is acceptable.** A Master's-level thesis can legitimately replicate published research; a doctorate cannot rest on replication.
- **Secondary sources are acceptable.** Citing an authority quoting another authority ("Francis gives several definitions of originality (Phillips & Pugh 2005)") is legitimate at Master's level. At doctoral level the candidate must have read and evaluated Francis in the original — Rule R4's read-in-full obligation is a *doctoral* license requirement; at Master's level, secondary citation is permitted but must be marked as such.
- **A full literature summary suffices;** it need not be the evaluative, critical review a doctorate requires. What a strong Master's review still shows: ability to test ideas, command of appropriate techniques, use of published work and source material, familiarity with competing theories and empirical studies.
- **What the doctorate adds:** greater depth, greater synthesis and critical ability, more detailed investigation of practical illustrations, and considerably more emphasis on original work.

## 3. Writing consequences by rule

- **Register rules (R1–R3) apply unchanged at every level.** Defensive writing, rigor-adverbs, and cliché are level-independent failures.
- **Traceability (R4):** unchanged in spirit; the secondary-source license above is the one Master's-level relaxation, and it requires explicit attribution of the chain ("as cited in").
- **Originality claims (F3, `originality-forms.md`):** at Master's level, forms 3 (executing work designed by the supervisor), 6 (originality in testing someone else's idea), 10 (context transfer), and 11 (technique transfer) are *fully sufficient* — not consolation prizes. Claiming form 15 (new framework/theory) in a Master's thesis triggers the overclaim attack and is unnecessary for the grade. The calibration rule: claim the form the evidence supports, and note that the required form is one or two rungs lower than doctoral writing habits suggest.
- **Contribution nouns and ordering (F1, F2):** apply unchanged; a Master's empirical study is still evidence-first, artifact-as-instrument second.
- **Claim strength:** the larger register risk at Master's level is overclaiming, not underclaiming. "This work provides evidence toward X" is an honest and sufficient Master's-level claim where a doctorate would need "this work establishes X." Do not import doctoral claim-strength into Master's prose.
- **Examiner stance ("judginess"):** a Master's examiner asks *was the research process executed to professional standard, and does the work show a degree of originality?* A doctoral examiner asks *is this an original contribution that extends the field, produced with full autonomy?* Anticipate the first set of questions in a Master's thesis: method justification, execution quality, correct analysis, honest limitations — the contribution argument carries proportionally less weight than in a doctorate.

## 4. Master's proposal norms

The doctoral proposal anatomy in `proposal-writing.md` §2 applies with these reductions:

- **Length:** typically 2–5 pages (institution-dependent; e.g., TU Wien informatics groups specify type of work, problem statement, methods, related work, and timeline for Master's proposals). Always defer to the institution's template.
- **Scale:** one study or one small study series; a design-and-evaluate arc (formative work → prototype → one summative study) is a complete Master's project. Feasibility horizon is roughly six months to a year of thesis work, not three to four years.
- **Required content:** problem statement, research question (the question-not-design-goal rule and the operational-goals criterion apply at full strength — they are level-independent), method(s) with a proportionate evaluation plan (one study specified properly beats three sketched), expected outcome, related work in brief, timeline.
- **Not required:** publication plan, success-criteria section, funding argument. Including a compact success-criteria line still strengthens the proposal.
- **Audience:** the supervisor and, at some institutions, a committee — the fit argument is about the supervisor's group specifically.
- *Verification status: the reductions above are working conventions synthesized from institutional Master's-proposal guidelines (TU Wien group-level guidance) and the doctoral sources, scaled down; no single authoritative cross-institution Master's-proposal standard exists. The institution's own template always wins.*
