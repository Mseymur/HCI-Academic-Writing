# Fifteen Recognized Forms of Originality (Phillips & Pugh)

Source: Estelle Phillips & Derek Pugh, *How to Get a PhD: A Handbook for Students and Their Supervisors*, Open University Press — the discussion of the concept of originality (ch. 6 in the 4th ed., 2005; retained as "15 ways to be original" in ch. 5 of the current edition, Phillips & Johnson). The early items are adapted by the authors from Francis (1976); the rest were gathered from their interviews with students, supervisors, and examiners.

*Verification status: the count (15) and individual items are triangulated from the book's partially accessible text, the University of Melbourne's official academic-skills guidance reproducing the list, and review coverage of the current edition quoting items verbatim. Item wording below is paraphrased, not quoted. If exact original wording is ever needed (e.g., for a citation in a thesis), consult the book directly.*

**Why this list exists in the skill:** doctoral originality is far humbler than outsiders expect. The book pairs this with a companion warning — *overestimating what is required* is one of the canonical ways of not getting a PhD. An "original contribution" does not mean a field-shaking breakthrough; it means one of the following, done to professional standard. Use the list in two directions: to reassure (the work already qualifies under item N) and to sharpen (name *which* form of originality is being claimed, then claim only that).

1. **Writing down a major piece of new information for the first time** — first documentation of something previously unrecorded.
2. **Continuing a previously original piece of work** — extending, qualifying, or elaborating an existing line of research.
3. **Carrying out original work designed by someone else** (typically the supervisor) — the design is theirs; the disciplined execution and interpretation are the candidate's.
4. **Providing a single original technique, observation, or result within an otherwise unoriginal but competent piece of research** — one genuinely new element carries the originality of the whole.
5. **Having many original ideas, methods, and interpretations, all performed by others under the direction of the postgraduate** — originality of research direction rather than of execution.
6. **Showing originality in testing somebody else's idea** — the idea is borrowed; the test of it is new.
7. **Carrying out empirical work that has not been done before** — new data from a study nobody has run.
8. **Making a synthesis that has not been made before** — combining bodies of knowledge that had not been brought together. (Synthesis of *knowledge* is a legitimate originality form; note the contrast with Rule F3 — bolting together existing *system components* and calling the assembly novel is the packaging claim examiners reject. The synthesis must produce an insight, not a parts list.)
9. **Using already-known material with a new interpretation** — reinterpretation of existing data, texts, or findings.
10. **Trying out in one country or context something previously done only elsewhere** — geographic or cultural transfer of an established study.
11. **Taking an existing technique and applying it in a new area** — methodological transfer.
12. **Bringing new evidence to bear on an old issue** — fresh data enters a standing debate.
13. **Being cross-disciplinary and using different methodologies** — importing another field's methods into one's own.
14. **Looking at areas that people in the discipline have not looked at** — an unstudied topic, population, or setting.
15. **Adding to knowledge in a way that has not been done before** — the residual category: a new theory, framework, model, or solution form.

**Usage rule:** when a draft claims originality, identify which numbered form the claim actually is, and phrase the claim at that scale. A thesis doing item 11 (technique transfer) that describes itself in item 15 language (new framework) hands the examiner a mismatch to attack. Most solid theses are items 2, 7, 8, 10, 11, or 12.
