# Writing an HCI Research Proposal

Source basis: CHI 2015 Doctoral Consortium proposal-content guide (read in full — the most detailed official HCI-venue specification of doctoral research descriptions); CHI 2022–2025 Doctoral Consortium requirements (official venue guidance, consistent across years); TU Wien Informatics doctoral-school procedure (official, representative of structured European doctorates); University of Vienna doctoral proposal guidance (official); UK university proposal guidance from Bristol (SPAIS), Sheffield (SPIR), and Edinburgh (official). Register and framing rules from SKILL.md apply throughout; this file adds the proposal-specific structure.

## 1. What a proposal is graded on

A thesis defends delivered contributions; a proposal argues that a question deserves years of work and that this candidate, at this institution, can answer it. Across the sources, assessors read for:

- **Originality of the question** — a real gap (see `novelty-and-positioning.md` §1–2), not a topic.
- **Coherence** — problem → question → goals → methods → contributions form one traceable chain.
- **Feasibility** — completable in the program period with the named resources.
- **Methodological awareness** — the right methods, matched to the questions, with their limits known.
- **Fit** — alignment with the supervisor's expertise and the department's research priorities.
- In structured European doctorates (TU Wien proficiency evaluation, ~0.5–1.5 years in): additionally **success criteria** and a **publication plan**. At that stage, having chosen "a clear and appropriate path" matters; having results does not.

This file describes the doctoral standard. For Master's proposals, the same anatomy applies at reduced scale with different licenses — read `level-calibration.md` §4 for the reductions and §2–§3 for what Master's examiners actually grade.

## 2. Section anatomy (HCI-specific, from the CHI Doctoral Consortium guides)

Order and naming can flex; every function below must be discharged somewhere.

### Context and motivation
Frame the domain, state the problem in general terms, and answer four questions (CHI DC's own list): What is the general area? How is it relevant to HCI? What motivates studying this particular problem? **Why will anyone care when the problem is solved?** The last question is Olsen's importance criterion in venue language — answer it before any gap claim (Rule F7). Anchor in the STU context: which situations, tasks, users.

### Background and related work
Brief and selective — enough to show command of what others have done and **how the proposed work builds on it**. The test is the build-upon relation, not coverage. Positioning rules apply at reduced length (Rule F6): themes, not papers; each theme closes on what remains open; the closest prior systems named and bounded precisely. A proposal-length review that cannot be traced into the gap statement is filler.

### Thesis or problem statement
One concise statement of the highest-level problem or position — a hypothesis, proposition, or conjecture — followed by the specific sub-problems that must be solved to satisfy it. Two tests (CHI DC): is the problem stated unambiguously, and is it worthy of a doctoral thesis (calibrate against `originality-forms.md`; most sound theses claim forms 2, 7, 8, 10, 11, or 12, not form 15). This is Rule F5 in proposal form: the storyline exists before the work does.

### Research questions vs. design goals
State research questions as questions — what will be *known* afterward that is not known now. A design goal ("develop a tool that supports X") is not a research question; it belongs inside the method as the instrument serving a question ("does X change how users perform Y, and under which conditions?"). A proposal led by design goals reads as an engineering plan and gets graded as one (Rule F1's rubric problem, one document earlier). One main question; sub-questions explicitly subordinate; none answerable yes/no.

### Research goals and methods — the operational-goals criterion
For each goal, state the method that achieves it. Goals must be **operational**: if the goal is later claimed as achieved, the delivered solution must be checkable against the goal statement. "Understand how users adopt X" is not operational; "identify which of the factors A, B, C predict abandonment of X within the first month, via a longitudinal diary study (n≈20)" is. CHI DC's own warning: when problems, goals, methods, and contributions are not clearly stated, readers cannot evaluate the solutions — vagueness here is the primary rejection cause. Each question–goal–method triple should be traceable in both directions.

### The evaluation plan (the section HCI expects and generic guides omit)
For every planned study, specify at proposal level: study type (controlled experiment, field study, interview study, survey, diary study...), design (between/within/mixed; conditions and counterbalancing where applicable), intended participants (population, approximate n with a power or saturation rationale stated plainly — one line, no precedent-stacking, Rule F4), measures (which instruments, which behavioral/performance metrics), analysis approach, and ethics/approval requirements. Where the work involves building: name what will be built, at what fidelity, and state explicitly that the prototype is the instrument for the studies (Rule F2 ordering). Distinguish formative work (studies that shape the design) from summative work (studies that answer the research questions); only summative studies discharge research questions. *The formative/summative framing and the per-study checklist are working conventions from HCI methods practice, not quoted from a single source; the requirement to specify methods per goal is CHI DC verbatim intent.*

### Work plan, timeline, resources
A realistic timeline with milestones mapped to the goals; required resources named (equipment, lab access, participant pools, data, training) with one line each on why. Scope boundaries framed as feasibility — "the project is scoped to X; Y is beyond the funded period" — not as apology (Rule R2). In Austrian/German-system doctorates the proposal's time-and-work plan becomes the basis of the formal thesis agreement (Dissertationsvereinbarung at TU Wien), so the plan must survive being taken literally.

### Publication plan (structured European doctorates)
Map the planned studies to intended publications and venues. Where a cumulative dissertation is the target (TU Wien: at least three first-author peer-reviewed publications plus an integrating chapter), the mapping from study → paper → thesis chapter is part of the proposal's feasibility argument, not an afterthought.

### Success criteria
State what "done and good" means per goal, checkable at evaluation time — the operational-goals criterion applied to the whole project. Required content in TU Wien-style proficiency evaluations; strengthens any proposal.

### Expected contributions
Connect back to the problem statement: what results or outcomes the dissertation will produce and what they contribute to the HCI community. Use the contribution-type vocabulary (`contribution-types.md`): name each expected contribution by type (empirical, artifact-as-instrument, methodological, guidelines), at the correct scale, in Rule F2 order — expected evidence first. Prospective and specific: "will provide the first controlled evidence of X under Y" — never "will contribute to knowledge in the field."

## 3. Length and register norms

- **Length:** admission proposals commonly 1,500–3,000 words (UK norms); Austrian doctoral proposals up to ~10 pages / 15,000–20,000 characters (University of Vienna); CHI DC research descriptions six pages excluding references. Always defer to the target institution's stated limit; exceeding it is read as inability to prioritize.
- **Register:** all Layer-1 rules apply unchanged. Prospective tense does not license hedging inflation — "will investigate," not "hopes to possibly explore." The proposal is a rigorous starting point expected to evolve under supervision, but it must read as viable exactly as written. It is not a begging letter: the stance is a researcher bringing the department a question worth its resources.
- **Self-check additions for proposals:** every research question stated as a question, none led by a design goal; every goal operational; every question–goal–method–contribution chain traceable both directions; evaluation plan specifies design, participants, measures, analysis per study; timeline, resources, and (where applicable) publication plan and success criteria present; contribution nouns and ordering correct; fit stated, not implied.
