# Novelty, Gap Statements, Related-Work Positioning, and Proposal Framing

Source basis: Swales, *Genre Analysis: English in Academic and Research Settings* (1990) — the CARS model, verified across multiple university writing-center guides (USC, IIT, Charles University) and excerpts of the model text; Olsen, UIST 2007 (read in full); Wobbrock 2012 whitepaper (read in full); official university proposal guidance from Bristol (SPAIS), Sheffield (SPIR), Edinburgh, and Maryland graduate programs.

## 1. The anatomy of a gap statement: Swales' CARS model

Nearly all research introductions perform three moves ("Create A Research Space"):

- **Move 1 — Establish a territory:** show the general area matters (claim centrality), make topic generalizations, review the key prior research. This is the importance argument; do it before any gap talk (cf. Rule F7 / Olsen's importance criterion).
- **Move 2 — Establish a niche:** show something is missing. Swales identifies four legitimate ways:
  1. **Counter-claiming** — prior work's conclusion is challenged with reasons.
  2. **Indicating a gap** — a question, comparison, population, or context the literature has not addressed.
  3. **Question-raising** — prior findings raise a question they do not answer.
  4. **Continuing a tradition** — extending an established, explicitly unfinished line of work.
- **Move 3 — Occupy the niche:** state what this work does — answer the question, fill the gap, test the counter-claim — and preview the contribution and structure.

**What is not on the Move-2 list:** "nobody has combined X + Y + Z." Component combination is not one of the recognized niche moves; it is a packaging claim (Rule F3). If the true situation is a combination, convert it into Move-2 form: what *question about people or performance* does the combined condition make answerable for the first time? State that question as the gap.

**Tone option for Move 2:** the niche can be established with neutral contrastive statements ("A has been established for X; whether it holds for Y is unexamined") rather than attacking prior work. In a Master's/PhD context, prefer the neutral form except where a genuine counter-claim is the contribution.

## 2. What examiners and reviewers accept as a novelty claim

Accepted shapes (each maps to a CARS Move-2 type and often to an originality form in `originality-forms.md`):
- An **open empirical question** the field has not answered (gap; forms 7, 12, 14).
- An **unmeasured comparison** — two conditions never compared under controlled conditions (gap; form 7).
- An **unoccupied cell in a design space** — the dimensions exist, the combination point has no study (gap; forms 8, 14). This is the legitimate cousin of the combination claim: the design space frames the cell as a *question*, not a parts list.
- A **transfer with a reason** — established technique or finding taken to a new area, population, or context, with an argument for why the transfer is non-obvious (continuing a tradition; forms 10, 11).
- A **counter-claim with evidence** — challenging a standing conclusion (counter-claim; forms 6, 9, 12).

Rejected shapes:
- **Component combinations** ("first to integrate X, Y, and Z") — engineering, not science.
- **"First to build X"** where X assembles known parts — the examiner names the prior systems holding each part.
- **Territory already occupied** — a "gap" that named prior systems in fact cover. The defense against this is pre-emptive honesty done declaratively: name the closest prior work and state precisely which question it answered and which it did not. Conceding occupied territory is what makes the remaining gap credible (Rule F3, not Rule R1 — this is stating facts, not arguing with ghosts).

**Incremental vs. conceptual novelty:** incremental novelty (transfer, extension, new evidence) is defended by precision — exactly what changes, exactly what was measured before, exactly what could not have been predicted. Conceptual novelty (new model, new framing) is defended by generativity and prediction — what it explains and foretells that existing accounts do not. Claiming conceptual novelty for incremental work triggers the mismatch attack; claiming incremental novelty for conceptual work undersells but survives. When in doubt, claim the smaller.

## 3. Related work that positions rather than lists

- **Organize by theme around the research question**, never paper-by-paper or chronologically. Each theme is a strand of the gap argument.
- **Every subsection ends on what remains open.** That closing sentence is a Move-2 statement; the section's final paragraph assembles these open ends into the gap the thesis fills. A reader should be able to reconstruct the gap statement from the subsection endings alone.
- **Cite to characterize, not to enumerate.** A source appears because the argument needs what it established or failed to establish. "Several studies have examined X [1–7]" without saying what they found is inventory, not positioning.
- **Name the closest competitors and be exact about them.** The strongest related-work sections give the most space to the most threatening prior work and draw the boundary precisely. Vagueness about close competitors reads as either ignorance or concealment — both worse than the competitor's existence.
- **The section is judged like a miniature survey contribution:** synthesis, organization, fairness (Wobbrock's survey criteria) — "not mere laundry lists."

## 5. Abstract and title framing (working conventions)

- **Abstract:** compressed CARS plus results — context (one or two sentences), gap (one), what was done and how (two or three), principal findings or, in a proposal, expected outcomes (one or two), contribution (one). Every sentence declarative; no citations; no "in this paper we..." padding beyond one occurrence; the contribution sentence uses the correct noun at the correct scale (Rules F1, F3).
- **Title:** names the question or the finding, plus the method or context where it disambiguates ("...: A Controlled Study of X in Y"). Titles claiming a framework or system commit the paper to that rubric (Rule F1) — title and contribution noun must agree.
